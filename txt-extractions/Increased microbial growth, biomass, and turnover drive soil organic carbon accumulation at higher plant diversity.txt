Grassland	ecosystems	are	predicted	to	experience	most	biodiversity	losses	as	a	consequence	 of	land‚Äêuse	change,	such	as	the	conversion	of	grasslands	into	croplands	(Sala	et	al.,	2000)	and	recent	studies	revealed	concomitant	 negative	impacts	on	soil	carbon	cycling	(Chen	&	Chen,	2019;	Tang	et	al.,	2019).	Terrestrial	ecosystems	store	most	organic	carbon	in	soils	where	it	has	the	670‚ÄÉ |‚ÄÉ ‚ÄÉ‚ÄÇPROMMER Et al.potential	to	become	stable	soil	carbon	and	thus	can	be	seques‚Äêtered	for	longer	time	periods.Globally,	terrestrial	carbon	storage	is	dominated	by	forests	(39%	of	the	total	terrestrial	organic	carbon	stored	in	forest	soils	and	veg‚Äêetation),	but	grasslands	also	contribute	substantially	 (34%	of	the	total	terrestrial	carbon)	as	they	cover	a	large	part	of	the	world‚Äôs	landmass,	with	~53	√ó	106	km2	grassland	area	versus	~29	√ó	106	km2 forest	area	(White,	Murray,	&	Rohweder,	2000).	Soil	organic	carbon	(SOC)	represents	the	largest	carbon	reservoir	in	global	grasslands,	with	up	to	98%	carbon	stored	belowground	 (Hungate	et	al.,	1997).	As	such,	understanding 	the	mechanisms	that	sustain	grassland	SOC	storage	is	of	utmost	importance	for	estimating	the	potential	of	grasslands	to	reduce	atmospheric	carbon	dioxide	(CO2)	concen‚Äêtrations	and	mitigate	feedbacks	from	the	biosphere	to	the	climate	system.Plant	diversity	is	increasingly	recognized	to	be	central	to	grass‚Äêland	SOC	storage,	with	observations	 from	biodiversity	experiments	demonstrating	 clear	links	between	plant	diversity,	primary	produc‚Äêtivity,	and	ecosystem	carbon	cycling	(Cong	et	al.,	2014;	De	Deyn	et	al.,	2011;	Fornara	&	Tilman,	2008;	Lange	et	al.,	2015;	Naeem,	Thompson,	Lawler,	Lawton,	&	Woodfin,	1994).Hereafter,	we	use	plant	diversity	as	a	term	to	describe	both	plant	species	number	and	functional	composition,	and	specify	when	referring	specifically	to	plant	species	richness	(PSR),	func‚Äêtional	group	richness,	or	functional	group	identity.	Higher	abo‚Äêveground	plant	productivity	as	a	consequence 	of	increased	plant	diversity	is	usually	also	accompanied	 by	increased	belowground 	plant	biomass	production,	although	the	latter	may	occur	only	after	a	delay	(Cong	et	al.,	2014;	Fornara	&	Tilman,	2008;	Ravenek	et	al.,	2014).	However,	while	there	is	evidence	that	increasing	plant	di‚Äêversity	translates	into	greater	aboveground	 primary	productivity	(Roscher	et	al.,	2005;	Spehn	et	al.,	2005;	Tilman,	Wedin,	&	Knops,	1996),	few	studies	have	investigated	the	mechanisms	linking	plant	diversity	and	plant	productivity	with	SOC	dynamics.	This	is	partly	due	to	the	paucity	of	long‚Äêterm	biodiversity	experiments	that	allow	for	exploration	of	typically	slow	changes	in	SOC	storage.	Indeed,	we	are	aware	of	only	four	of	such	grassland	biodiversity	experi‚Äêments	globally.	Studies	from	these	experiments	have	consistently	shown	positive	effects	of	plant	diversity	on	SOC	storage,	and	have	largely	ascribed	this	to	increased	rhizosphere	carbon	inputs	(Cong	et	al.,	2014;	De	Deyn	et	al.,	2011;	Fornara	&	Tilman,	2008;	Lange	 et	al.,	2015;	Steinbeiss,	Be√üler,	et	al.,	2008).	Yet,	how	this	mecha‚Äênism	is	linked	to	microbial	carbon	processing	has	rarely	been	em‚Äêpirically	tested,	limiting	our	ability	to	implement	microbial	carbon	dynamics	in	climate‚Äêcarbon 	models	and	dynamic	global	vegetation	models	(Crowther	et	al.,	2016).The	build‚Äêup	of	organic	carbon	ultimately	depends	on	the	bal‚Äêance	between	carbon	inputs	and	outputs	from	the	system,	which	is	determined	by	plant	biomass	production,	and	SOC	formation	and	decomposition,	 and	is	therefore,	to	a	high	degree,	governed	by	the	activity	of	soil	microbes.	Most	plant‚Äêderived	 carbon	is	taken	up	by	soil	microbes	and	used	to	either	generate	energy	(and	thus	CO2)	or	generate	biomass.	After	death,	microbial	necromass	becomes	part	of	the	nonliving	soil	organic	matter	pool	(Miltner,	Bombach,	Schmidt‚ÄêBrucken,	&	Kastner,	2012).	Estimates	of	the	proportion	of	microbi‚Äêally	derived	carbon	transformed	into	nonliving	SOC	range	from	40%	(Kindler,	Miltner,	Richnow,	&	Kastner,	2006)	to	80%	(Liang	&	Balser,	2011),	but	the	role	of	necromass	carbon	for	SOC	build‚Äêup	is	not	well	tested	in	the	context	of	changing	PSR.	It	is,	therefore,	important	to	distinguish	between	microbial	catabolic	and	anabolic	pathways	in	order	to	disentangle	their	specific	contributions	to	SOC	accumula‚Äêtion.	One	way	to	synthesize	microbial	physiology	is	the	widely	used	metric	of	microbial	carbon	use	efficiency	(CUE),	which	describes	the	efficiency	by	which	microbes	convert	organic	carbon	into	growth	(Manzoni,	Taylor,	Richter,	Porporato,	&	Agren,	2012;	Sinsabaugh,	Manzoni,	Moorhead,	&	Richter,	2013).	When	incorporated	 into	mi‚Äêcrobial	biomass,	carbon	has	the	potential	to	become	part	of	the	soil	organic	matter	pool	and	can	reside	in	soils	for	longer	time	periods.	Accordingly,	a	high	microbial	CUE	favors	SOC	storage,	although	other	physiological	 characteristics	 of	the	soil	microbial	community	like	microbial	growth	and	turnover	may	equally	promote	SOC	ac‚Äêcumulation.	Moreover,	microbial	CUE	was	shown	to	scale	positively	with	microbial	growth	(Zheng	et	al.,	2019)	and	to	be	maximized	at	highest	growth	rates	(Manzoni	et	al.,	2017).	Nevertheless,	 despite	the	general	importance	of	these	microbial	processes	to	SOC	accu‚Äêmulation,	their	relationships	with	plant	diversity	are	almost	entirely	unknown.In	this	study,	we	explicitly	addressed	the	question	of	how	soil	microbial	physiology	responds	to	increasing	plant	diversity.	PSR,	functional	group	richness,	and	functional	composition	have	all	been	shown	to	promote	aboveground 	and	belowground 	plant	productiv‚Äêity	in	the	Jena	Experiment	(Marquard	et	al.,	2009;	Ravenek	et	al.,	2014).	However,	increases	in	root	biomass	were	more	strongly	de‚Äêtermined	by	PSR	than	by	functional	group	richness	(Ravenek	et	al.,	2014)	and	further	led	to	greater	rhizosphere	carbon	inputs	in	high‚Äêdiversity	plant	communities	 (Chen	et	al.,	2017;	Lange	et	al.,	2015).	We	here	focus	on	how	microbial	activity	impacts	the	transforma‚Äêtion	of	detrital	organic	material	to	unravel	the	causal	physiological	pathways	through	which	soil	microbes	promote	SOC	accumulation 	(Figure	1).	Specifically,	we	used	the	long‚Äêterm	biodiversity	exper‚Äêiment	in	Jena	(Roscher	et	al.,	2004)	to	measure	gross	rates	of	mi‚Äêcrobial	community	growth,	turnover,	and	CUE	in	grassland	plots	differing	in	plant	diversity. The	overall	model	fit	was	assessed	using	Shipley's	test	of	directed	sep‚Äêaration,	for	which	a	good	model	fit	is	obtained	when	Fisher's	C	is	statistically	nonsignificant 	(p	>	.05)	(Shipley,	2009).	Furthermore, 	as	the	 piecewiseSEM 	package	reports	missing	or	incomplete	path‚Äêways,	such	pathways	were	tested	in	parallel	models	and	included	in	the	model	if	the	respective	pathway	was	statistically	significant	(p	<	.05)	and	mechanistically 	meaningful. Models	constructed	in 	this	manner	differ	only	in	the	linkage	of	pathways	but	contain	the	same	dataset	and	thus	can	be	compared	using	the	Akaike	infor‚Äêmation	criterion	(AIC)	and	the	AICc	(for	small	sample	sizes).
